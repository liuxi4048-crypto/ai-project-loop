Schools should require students to learn a second language, and there are several strong reasons for this position.

First, learning another language brings a clear cognitive benefit. Because the brain must switch between systems of grammar and vocabulary, students often improve their memory and problem-solving skills. Moreover, research suggests that bilingual students frequently outperform their peers on tasks that demand focus and flexibility.

Second, a second language opens doors in a student's future career. As businesses become more global, employers increasingly value candidates who can communicate across borders. Therefore, a graduate who speaks two languages holds a real advantage in the job market.

Finally, studying a language exposes students to another culture. However, this exposure does more than teach words; it builds empathy and an appreciation for people whose lives differ from our own. For example, reading a story in its original language reveals ideas that translation can blur.

In conclusion, because the benefits reach into thinking, work, and culture, schools should treat a second language not as an option but as a requirement.
